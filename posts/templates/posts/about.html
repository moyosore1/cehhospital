Centre for Innovations in Eye Care Health

Our Declaration, Our Pact with Nigerians.

We shall not be limited by traditions. We shall breakdown existing barriers and bring modern technology to bear through our Centre for Innovations in Eye Care.

A centre that will provide an uninterrupted qualitative, affordable, accessible and efficient eye care services for all who seek help with compassion and love irrespective of socio- economic status, race or creed; provide high quality clinical and surgical services for residency and short term fellowship training; deliver sustainable, comprehensive eye care services through promotive, curative and preventive eye care practice, and an alternative platform for training of eye care team that focuses not only on the cognitive and skill, but also an attitude, effective outreach programmes reaching out to the neglected and underprivileged people and operational research and innovation for the improvement of eye care in the country.

Our Vision at Eleta Eye Institute Group To become the leading and best eye care provider on the African continent that will demonstrate excellence in service, in training, in research and a strong commitment to innovation for the improvement of eye care for the benefit of humanity and the greater glory of God.

Naked Facts about eye care in Nigeria that you must know.

  • There are over 4 million visually impaired adults in Nigeria. About 1.3 million aged 40 years and over are BLIND. Another 3.5 million adults are estimated to have moderate to severe visual impairment. (Extrapolated from Nigerian National Survey of Blindness and Low Vision) In real terms, you can imagine 8 out of 10 people living in Oyo State, or half the population of Lagos State or the entire population Ogun State or Osun State being blind or visually impaired!

  • Cataract is the single commonest cause of blindness and about 750,000 adults are in immediate need of cataract surgery.

  • There are insufficient numbers of ophthalmologists and eye care facilities for service and training in Nigeria. Patients have to travel distances of between 50 to 400 kilometres to access eye care.

  • A good number of all cataract procedures are still performed by quacks adding to the number of people with irreversible blindness.

  • Glaucoma is the second leading cause of blindness But unlike cataract,
    - The sufferer has no symptoms until the very late stages;
    - It is not easily recognizable in the early stages,
    - Difficult to diagnose until damage has begun except by trained medical experts
    - Blindness is irreversible.

  • Many top business executives live with glaucoma without knowing and when it finally announces its presence to them, it is often too late.

  • If all the people who would like to know whether they have glaucoma come to hospital it would take the number of ophthalmologists in the country 77years, working 365days in a year to see them.

  • There are less than 400 active ophthalmologists (eye medical doctors) in the Nigeria and majority are in the Teaching Hospitals and 8 big cities. That means in the event of an emergency, your chances of being attended to by a qualified eye medical doctor outside the big cities are very slim. The poor people and those living in rural areas can consider themselves blind.

  • Children are likely to go blind with simple diseases such as cataract and infection because they have special needs which are even more difficult to meet.

  • Nigeria needs about 2000 ophthalmologists and hope of getting any close to this figure is not insight because only about 35 new ophthalmologists pass out of the training programme every year. This is just at replacement level with no room for growth.

  • Training of Ophthalmologists is done mainly in Federal and State government Teaching Hospitals and in addition the trainees are greatly disadvantaged because of lack of appropriate and up-to-date equipment and low volume of surgical patients which make training impossible at international standards

  • Thus there is need to provide an alternative platforms for comprehensive training of ophthalmologists and other eye care workers that focuses not only on the cognitive and skill, but also on attitude, leadership and work ethics. Many such private initiatives are needed.

  • Research into finding cures for common ailments which afflict us such as glaucoma or finding new ways of solving our peculiar eye care challenges are either rudimentary or non-existent. Thus we are a consumer nation in its entire ramification, depending solely on the outcome of various researches from outside our borders
